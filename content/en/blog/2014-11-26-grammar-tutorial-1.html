A remark beforehand: I'm not a linguist; I am just interested in how languages work. Also, I am not a native English speaker. If you see errors, please contact me so that I can correct them. Thanks :)

Nouns and verbs

Let's first look at some very basic sentences.

(3)The man goes to the market.

(4)I see John.

(5)I swim in the river.

We can hopefully agree that those sentences are valid English. When we look at them more closely, we see that there are various types of words that take on different functions in the sentence. There are several words (man, market, John, river) that denote things, persons or places; in other words, objects. Other words (goes, see, swim) denote the action that is happening. Finally there are some small words (I, to, the, in) that belong to neither category.

The words noun and verb, like many linguistic terms, are derived from Latin.

In the remainder, we will see that those ‘categories’ are very relevant to learn more about. Let's give them names: John, market, fish and river are called nouns and go, see and swims are called verbs.

Almost all languages have a noun/verb-distinction, but it is not really necessary. The invented language Lojban makes no difference between nouns and verbs.

Now unfortunately, to make things complicated, the English language has the annoying habit of having many words that can be both noun or verb. For example, consider the following example:

(6)I went for a swim in the river.

There swim suddenly became a noun. Compare sentence (5) and (6) to see what is happening: in (5), the word swim indicates the main action, while in (6) it denotes an (abstract) object, namely an instance of swimming. You see that while the noun swim and the verb swim have essentially the same meaning – both indicate that some person is making all kinds of strange movements while in the water – the difference between the noun and the verb is how they are used in the sentence. A verb is used to denote the main action in a sentence; a noun is used to denote objects.

Proper names like John do not get an article, but they are still considered nouns.

Another way to look at this is the following. A noun can almost always be preceded by an article, which is the word a(n) or the. On the other hand, an article can never occur before a verb.
